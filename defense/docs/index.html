The U.S. government spends hundreds of billions each year on defense. Where does it go?

Author

Sam Gauntt

Published

2026-06-17 12:01

What is this story about?

The federal government sends hundreds of billions in defense spending to states across the U.S. each year. Who benefits the most? This story will look into where DOD spending is concentrated, and how population, political ideology and other traits relate to this spread.

Why tell this story NOW?

With President Trump requesting $1.5 trillion for the next federal military budget, an increase of 44%, now is a better time than ever to dig into where the federal government concentrates its military spending. 

Key takeaways

Finding 1: Texas, California and Virginia made up the Top 3 states in total spending in both 2020 and 2024. But they switched positions.

Code 
library(tidyverse)
library(tidycensus)
library(janitor)
library(DT) 
library(dplyr) 

spending <- read_csv("../data/dod_spending_fy2020-24.csv") |> 
     clean_names()

top_spenders_20 <- spending |>
  filter(fyear == 2020) |>
  group_by(state_nm) |>
  summarize(
    total_spent = sum(spending_total, na.rm = TRUE),
    .groups = "drop"
  ) |>
  arrange(desc(total_spent)) |>
  mutate(
    total_spent_billions = total_spent / 1e9,
    total_spending_20 = scales::dollar(total_spent_billions, suffix = "B")
  ) |> mutate(
    state_nm = replace_na(state_nm, "NO STATE LISTED")
  ) |>
  select(state_nm, total_spending_20) |> rename(
     State = state_nm,
     `Total defense spending in (billions)` = total_spending_20
   ) 

top_spenders_24 <- spending |>
  filter(fyear == 2024) |>
  group_by(state_nm) |>
  summarize(
    total_spent = sum(spending_total, na.rm = TRUE),
    .groups = "drop"
  ) |>
  arrange(desc(total_spent)) |>
  mutate(
    total_spent_billions = total_spent / 1e9,
    total_spending_24 = scales::dollar(total_spent_billions, suffix = "B")
  ) |>  mutate(
    state_nm = replace_na(state_nm, "NO STATE LISTED")
  ) |>select(state_nm, total_spending_24) |> rename(
     State = state_nm,
     `Total defense spending in (billions)` = total_spending_24
   )

Example employers

Palantir

My community is all the communities that States Newsroom serves, so it would be important to watch a national player. While there is a slew of major defense contractors receiving money from the government, perhaps none have raised more attention in recent years than Palantir. The company specializes in AI development and software engineering, and has drawn scrutiny for its creation of spyware and surveillance technology employed by the federal government. It has received many multi-billion contracts by the DOD/DOW.

Department of Defense contract announcement

This company is important to watch both for its controversial work, but also as a canary for the shifting federal defense contractor environment and increased presence of surveillance and technology companies in addition to more traditional weapons and vehicle manufacturers.

Lockheed Martin

A more traditional example of the kind of company receiving this spending is Lockheed Martin, the international defense contractor that manufactures fighter jets, missile and weapons systems, and spacecraft. The company generates about $75B in annual revenue, primarily through large government contracts. It has a close relationship with the U.S. government, employing about 110,000 workers across more than 150 facilities in dozens of states. This gives the company significant influence and lobbying power over Congress and the federal government,

Boeing

Boeing similarly conducts significant business with the federal government and U.S. military. In addition to its significant commercial business producing airliners and other passenger jets, it produces fighter jets, drones, missiles, tankers and various other military-focused platforms. It reached almost $90B in revenue last year, with about a third from government contracts.

Story impact

Trump’s request of $1.5 trillion in funding for the military would be a massive surge over any previous budget. It also coincides with slashes in federal support for many other agencies and departments, rising costs, increasing inflation and layoffs of federal workers. The money for this budget increase would have to come from other sources, such as cuts for other services. And this could impact daily lives of U.S. citizens.
